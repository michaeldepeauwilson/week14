How New York is the most Conservative City on Water

By Michael H. Wilson

More People Soaking in Less Water

New York is one of the most conservative big cities in the country in at least one category: water use. In a thirty-year period, New Yorker's water consumption steadily declined by more than more than 30 percent, according to data from OpenNYC.

Over the 30 years between 1979 and 2009, total consumption of water in the city dropped while the total number of New Yorkers rose by more than one million. The drop of water consumption in the face of strong growth shows a quick and deliberate move toward saving water across the city, and several factors contributed to it.

The first is improvement in home appliance technology such as water efficient toilets and less leaky plumbing. The Department of Environmental Protection has also launched series of conservation awareness over those years that resemble current programs like the toilet replacement initiative or Water for the Future program. The result is a savings of more than 75 gallons of water per person over three decades.

Conservative Now and into the Future

New York is continuing to make strides with public awareness and infrastructure that promote water conservative. The rapid decline of overall and per capita water use has become a tradition of the big city. The Water for the Future program is also starting construction for repairs this year on two major aqueducts, Catskill and Queens, which supply the drinking water for the city. The repairs are aimed at reducing waste of the water during transportation. In the meantime, New Yorkers will continue to enjoy one of the most conservative water systems in the country.
